Most documentation problems aren't writing problems. They're *structure* problems.

Users can't find what they need. Writers aren't sure what to write. The help center grows without a plan, and eventually becomes a labyrinth that nobody can navigate — not even the people who built it.

The Diátaxis framework changed how I approach all of this.

<!-- truncate -->

## What Diátaxis actually is

Diátaxis (from the Greek for "arrangement") is a framework for organising documentation into four distinct types:

| Type | Purpose | User need |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Tutorial** | Learning-oriented | "Take me through it step by step" |
| **How-To Guide** | Task-oriented | "Show me how to do X" |
| **Explanation** | Understanding-oriented | "Help me understand why" |
| **Reference** | Information-oriented | "What are the exact specs?" |

The insight is simple but powerful: these four types serve *completely different user needs*, and mixing them up is what causes most documentation to fail.

## Why I adopted it at Kovai.co

When I audited our documentation after joining as Lead, I found the classic problem: pages that tried to do everything at once. A "Getting Started" guide that was half tutorial, half reference, half explanation. Users were confused. Support tickets were high for topics we had supposedly documented.

I restructured the docs using Diátaxis principles over two quarters. The results:

- Reduced average time-on-page for task-based content (users found answers faster)
- Support queries for documented features dropped
- Writers had a clear decision framework — no more "what kind of doc should I write?"

## How I apply it in practice

### Start with user intent

Before writing anything, I ask: *What does the user want to accomplish right now?*

- Learning a product for the first time → Tutorial
- Completing a specific task → How-To Guide
- Understanding a concept or decision → Explanation
- Looking up a specific value/parameter → Reference

### Use it during content audits

Diátaxis is a fantastic audit tool. Go through existing docs and tag each page with its type. Pages that don't fit cleanly into one category are usually the ones causing confusion.

### Brief your team with it

When I onboard new writers, Diátaxis is one of the first frameworks I introduce. It gives everyone a shared vocabulary: *"This needs to be a How-To, not a Tutorial"* is a meaningful editorial note.

## Common mistakes

The most common trap is writing **hybrid documents** — a how-to that explains *why* too much, or a tutorial that becomes a reference. Guard against this by keeping the user's single intention in focus for each document.

The second mistake is treating Diátaxis as a rigid rule rather than a thinking tool. It's a compass, not a cage.
